[assistant] Good instinct to pause before splitting. Most "the monolith is slow" problems are not actually solved by microservices — and microservices add a large, permanent tax. Here's how I'd think about it.

## First: is slowness even an architecture problem?

Before any split, rule out the cheap wins, because microservices won't fix these — they'll hide them behind network calls and make them worse:

- **Database is the usual culprit.** N+1 queries (check with Django Debug Toolbar or `django-silk`), missing indexes, unbounded querysets, `SELECT *` on wide tables. `select_related`/`prefetch_related` and a few indexes routinely produce 10–100x wins.
- **No caching layer.** Redis/Memcached for expensive queries, `cached_property`, per-view or template fragment caching.
- **Synchronous work that should be async.** Move email, PDF generation, third-party API calls, etc. to Celery.
- **Under-provisioned or misconfigured.** Gunicorn worker count, DB connection pooling (PgBouncer), read replicas for read-heavy loads.
- **No profiling data.** If you can't point to *which* endpoints/queries are slow, you're not ready to re-architect. Get APM (Sentry, Datadog, New Relic) first.

A read replica + query fixes + Celery + caching will take a Django monolith very far. Many "we need microservices" situations end here.

## If it's genuinely an architecture problem, weigh these factors

**1. What's the actual driver?**
- *Scaling* — is the whole app slow, or one component (e.g., a reporting engine) with a different resource profile that you want to scale independently? That's a legitimate reason, but often solvable by extracting just that one service, not a full decomposition.
- *Team/organizational* — are multiple teams stepping on each other, blocked on shared deploys? This is the *strongest* real reason to split (Conway's Law). Microservices are an org-scaling tool at least as much as a performance one.
- If the driver is "the code is a mess," splitting a messy monolith gives you a *distributed* mess, which is strictly harder.

**2. Do clean domain boundaries exist?**
Can you identify bounded contexts with minimal coupling? If your models are a tangle of cross-domain foreign keys, you can't cleanly split — you'll spend the split turning in-process joins into chatty network calls and distributed transactions. **Enforce module boundaries inside the monolith first** (a "modular monolith") — separate Django apps with explicit, one-directional interfaces. If you can't draw the lines in one codebase, you can't draw them across services.

**3. Data ownership.** Each service owning its own data is the hard part. A shared database is a distributed monolith (worst of both). Splitting means giving up cross-service JOINs and ACID transactions — you inherit eventual consistency, sagas, and data duplication. This is usually the single biggest cost people underestimate.

**4. Operational maturity — the real price of admission.** Do you have: CI/CD per service, container orchestration, centralized logging, distributed tracing, service discovery, per-service monitoring/alerting, and on-call? Microservices trade code complexity for operational complexity. Without this platform investment, you'll be slower and less reliable, not faster.

**5. Team size and shape.** A rough rule: if you don't have enough engineers to give each service a clear owner (say, 2+ teams that are genuinely blocking each other), the coordination overhead outweighs the benefit. A 5-person team running 12 services is a common failure mode.

## What I'd recommend

1. **Profile and fix the cheap things first** — you may buy years of runway in a week.
2. **If boundaries are the problem, do a modular monolith** — strict app boundaries, no cross-domain ORM reaching, service-layer interfaces. This gets you 80% of the decoupling benefit at ~10% of the operational cost, and it's the necessary prerequisite anyway.
3. **If you still need to split, extract one service** — pick the component with the clearest boundary and the strongest independent-scaling or team-ownership case (the strangler-fig pattern). Learn the operational tax on one service before committing the whole system.

The honest default: **don't split yet.** Splitting is reversible in theory and brutal in practice. Make the monolith modular and fast first; extract services only when a specific boundary is causing specific, measured pain that in-process changes can't fix.
