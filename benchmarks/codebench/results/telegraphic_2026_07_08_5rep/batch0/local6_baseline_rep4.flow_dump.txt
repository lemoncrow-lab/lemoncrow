[assistant] Good instinct to interrogate this before committing—microservices solve organizational and scaling problems, but they introduce distributed-systems complexity that often makes a slow monolith *slower to work on*, not faster. Here's how I'd think about it.

## First: is "slow" actually a decomposition problem?

Most Django monolith slowness has nothing to do with being a monolith. Before splitting, rule these out—they're cheaper and usually the real culprit:

- **N+1 queries** — the #1 Django killer. Audit with `django-debug-toolbar` or `nplusone`; fix with `select_related`/`prefetch_related`.
- **Missing DB indexes** and slow queries — check `pg_stat_statements`.
- **No caching layer** — Redis/Memcached for expensive reads.
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery (email, PDF, third-party API calls).
- **Under-provisioned DB or app servers** — sometimes it's just a bigger box.

If you haven't profiled, do that first. Splitting the monolith fixes *none* of the above and adds network latency on top.

## If the problem is real, what factors actually matter?

**1. Are your bottlenecks isolable?**
Microservices help when a *specific* component has a *distinct* scaling profile (e.g., a CPU-heavy recommendation engine drowning out cheap CRUD requests). If load is uniform, you can't fix it by splitting—you'd scale every service equally, which is just a distributed monolith with worse latency.

**2. The database is the hard part, not the code.**
Splitting Python is easy. Splitting a shared Postgres schema is where projects die. Ask: can you draw clean data ownership boundaries where each service owns its tables and *nothing* else touches them? If services still need to JOIN across each other's data, you don't have a service boundary—you have a distributed join, which is slower and more fragile than a local one.

**3. Team topology.**
Microservices are primarily an *organizational* tool (Conway's Law). They pay off when you have multiple teams stepping on each other in one codebase/deploy pipeline. One team of 5? The coordination overhead of many repos, CI pipelines, and deploys will likely cost you more than it saves.

**4. Operational maturity.**
Distributed systems demand: centralized logging/tracing, service discovery, per-service monitoring, handling partial failures and retries, distributed transactions (or embracing eventual consistency), versioned APIs. If you don't have solid observability on the monolith today, you're not ready to debug ten services.

**5. Transaction boundaries.**
Django gives you free ACID transactions across your whole model graph. Cross a service boundary and that `transaction.atomic()` guarantee is gone—you're now writing sagas and compensating actions. Look hard at your write paths that span would-be-service boundaries.

## The pragmatic middle path

You rarely have to choose monolith vs. microservices:

1. **Profile and fix the actual slowness** (usually queries + caching + Celery). This alone resolves most cases.
2. **Modularize the monolith first** — enforce clean boundaries between Django apps *in-process* (explicit interfaces, no cross-app model imports, "modular monolith"). This is where the real design work is, and it's reversible.
3. **Extract one service** only if a specific component has a genuinely different scaling or team-ownership need—and only after step 2 proves the boundary is clean.

If you can't cleanly modularize the monolith, you *definitely* can't cleanly split it into services—you'll just get a distributed monolith: all the network latency and operational cost, none of the independence.
