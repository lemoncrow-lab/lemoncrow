[assistant] This is one of those decisions that's much easier to get wrong than right, so it's worth slowing down before splitting anything. Here are the key factors I'd weigh.

## First: is the monolith actually the problem?

Most "slow Django app" problems are not solved by microservices — and microservices will make them *worse* if the root cause is unaddressed. Before anything architectural, confirm where the slowness lives:

- **N+1 queries** — the #1 Django performance killer. Check `select_related`/`prefetch_related`, add `django-debug-toolbar` or `nplusone`.
- **Missing DB indexes** and slow queries — turn on Postgres `pg_stat_statements`.
- **No caching layer** — Redis/Memcached for expensive reads.
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery (email, reports, image processing).
- **Under-provisioned DB or single web worker** — sometimes it's just infra.

A modular monolith with these fixed handles enormous scale. Splitting into services adds network latency to every call that used to be an in-process function invocation — you can easily make a slow app *slower*.

## If it's genuinely an architectural ceiling, evaluate these factors

**1. Where are the real bottlenecks — and can you scale them independently?**
The strongest case for extraction is a component with a *different scaling profile*: e.g., a CPU-heavy report generator or ML inference path drowning out your fast CRUD endpoints. That's a candidate to peel off. If everything scales together, you don't need separate services.

**2. Data ownership and the database boundary.**
This is the hardest part, not the code. In a Django monolith you have foreign keys and cross-table joins everywhere. A service split means those become network calls or duplicated data, and you lose ACID transactions across the boundary — hello eventual consistency, distributed sagas, and reconciliation logic. If two candidate services can't cleanly own separate data, they aren't separate services.

**3. Domain boundaries (bounded contexts).**
Split along business capabilities that change independently (billing, catalog, notifications), not technical layers. A good sign: teams can deploy their piece without coordinating with others. If a typical feature touches three "services," your boundaries are wrong.

**4. Team size and org structure (Conway's Law).**
Microservices are as much an org solution as a technical one — they let independent teams ship independently. With one small team, you'll pay all the distributed-systems tax and get none of the autonomy benefit. Rule of thumb: if you don't have multiple teams stepping on each other in the codebase, you probably don't need services yet.

**5. Operational maturity you'll be forced to build.**
Distributed systems demand infrastructure a monolith doesn't: service discovery, centralized logging/tracing (distributed tracing becomes mandatory), per-service CI/CD, monitoring/alerting, versioned APIs, retries/circuit breakers, and handling partial failure. Honestly assess whether the team has (or wants to build) this.

**6. Latency and failure semantics.**
In-process calls are nanoseconds and never "half-fail." Network calls add milliseconds each and can time out, so a request fanning across services multiplies both latency and failure probability. You'll need timeouts, retries, idempotency, and graceful degradation everywhere.

## A pragmatic middle path

Consider a **modular monolith** first: enforce internal boundaries (Django apps with clear interfaces, no cross-app model reach-in, a linter/import rules to keep them honest). You get most of the maintainability wins with none of the network/ops cost — and it becomes the natural seam if you *do* later extract a service.

If you do split, use the **strangler fig pattern**: extract *one* well-bounded, independently-scaling service first (a clear win with a clean data boundary), learn the operational lessons on a low-risk piece, then decide whether to continue. Don't rewrite; carve incrementally.

## The decision, in one line

Split when a **specific bounded context** has a **different scaling need**, **independent data**, and an **independent team** — and when you've already ruled out the boring performance fixes. If any of those three is missing, you'll likely get a "distributed monolith": all the complexity of microservices with none of the independence.
