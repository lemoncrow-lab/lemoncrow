[assistant] Good instinct to pause before committing. Microservices solve organizational and scaling problems, but they add distributed-systems complexity that often makes a slow monolith *slower to work with*, not faster. Here's how I'd frame the decision.

## First: is "slow" a reason to split at all?

Splitting rarely fixes performance directly — you're adding network hops, serialization, and remote calls where you had in-process function calls. Before anything architectural, find out *why* it's slow:

- **Profile real requests** (Django Silk, `django-debug-toolbar`, or an APM like Datadog/Sentry). Slowness is usually one of: N+1 queries, missing DB indexes, unbounded querysets, synchronous work that should be a background job, or no caching.
- **Check the database first.** 80% of "the monolith is slow" turns out to be the ORM. `select_related`/`prefetch_related`, indexes, and query-count budgets often reclaim more than a rewrite.
- **Offload slow work** to Celery/RQ instead of blocking the request cycle.

If you haven't done this, do it first. It's days of work versus months, and you keep a single deployable.

## If it's genuinely an architecture/scaling problem, weigh these factors

**1. Team & organizational structure (the real driver).** Microservices pay off when independent teams need to deploy independently without coordinating. One team of 5–10 on one codebase almost never benefits — you get the costs (distributed debugging, versioned APIs, infra overhead) without the payoff. Conway's Law cuts both ways.

**2. Do clean domain boundaries exist?** Can you point to parts of the app that rarely change together and share little data? If everything touches the same core models (users, orders, billing all deeply intertwined), extraction will be painful and you'll end up with a "distributed monolith" — the worst of both worlds, where a change still requires deploying five services in lockstep.

**3. Data ownership.** This is the hardest part. Each service ideally owns its data. Django makes cross-app queries and joins trivial today; across services they become API calls or event streams. You lose ACID transactions across boundaries and inherit eventual consistency, sagas, and reconciliation logic.

**4. Operational maturity.** Do you have CI/CD, containerization, centralized logging, distributed tracing, service discovery, and on-call practices? Microservices multiply your deployment and observability surface. Without this foundation, you're trading a performance problem for an operations problem.

**5. What actually needs to scale?** Usually one or two hotspots (e.g., a reporting endpoint, image processing, a public API). You can often scale those independently *without* full microservices.

## The pragmatic middle ground

Most teams should consider these before microservices:

- **Optimize the monolith** — the profiling work above.
- **Modular monolith** — enforce internal boundaries (separate Django apps with explicit interfaces, no cross-app model imports) while keeping one deployable. This gets you most of the maintainability benefit and makes future extraction *possible* if you ever need it.
- **Extract one service** — the strangler-fig pattern. Pull out a single well-bounded, genuinely-independent piece (e.g., notifications, search, PDF generation). Learn the operational cost on something low-risk before betting the architecture on it.
- **Scale horizontally** — run more instances of the monolith behind a load balancer; add read replicas and caching (Redis). Django scales this way surprisingly far.

## A decision checklist

Ask the team:
1. Have we profiled and know *specifically* what's slow?
2. Is the pain **performance**, or is it actually **team velocity / merge conflicts / deploy coupling**? (These point to different solutions.)
3. Can we name 2–3 clean boundaries with minimal shared data?
4. Do we have the ops maturity to run distributed systems today?
5. Could a modular monolith + targeted optimization solve this for 20% of the effort?

If the honest answers are "no, we haven't profiled" and "it's really about performance" — start there. If it's "our 4 teams keep blocking each other on deploys and we have clear domain seams" — then a measured extraction (not a big-bang rewrite) makes sense.
