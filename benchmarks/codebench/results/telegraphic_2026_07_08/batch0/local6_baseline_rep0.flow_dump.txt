[assistant] Good instinct to pause and analyze before committing to microservices — the split is often blamed for problems that turn out to be fixable in the monolith, and it's much harder to reverse than to undo. Here are the key factors I'd weigh.

## First: is the monolith actually the problem?

"Slow" has many causes, and most of them don't require splitting anything. Before architecture, confirm *what* is slow:

- **Database** — N+1 queries, missing indexes, no `select_related`/`prefetch_related`, unbounded querysets. This is the #1 cause of slow Django apps and microservices won't fix it — you'll just have the same slow queries behind an HTTP hop.
- **No caching** — missing per-view/queryset caching, no Redis/Memcached layer.
- **Synchronous work in the request cycle** — emails, image processing, external API calls that belong in Celery/RQ.
- **Under-provisioned or single-process serving** — wrong Gunicorn/uWSGI worker count, no connection pooling.
- **No profiling** — before anything, get `django-silk` or APM (Datadog/Sentry Performance/New Relic) in and find the actual hot paths.

If you haven't profiled, do that first. Microservices frequently get proposed to fix problems that a week of query optimization and a Redis cache would solve — at a fraction of the cost and risk.

## If it's genuinely an architecture/scaling problem, the real factors:

**1. Team & organizational structure (Conway's Law).** Microservices are as much an org strategy as a technical one. If you have 2–3 teams that keep stepping on each other in the same codebase, service boundaries that match team boundaries can genuinely help. If you're one team of five, microservices mostly add overhead you'll feel daily.

**2. Do clear domain boundaries even exist?** Splitting a tangled monolith produces a *distributed* tangled monolith — the worst of both worlds. Can you draw bounded contexts (billing, catalog, notifications, auth) with minimal chatter between them? If everything touches everything, you're not ready.

**3. Data ownership and the database.** This is the hardest part. Django's ORM, migrations, and cross-table joins assume one database. Splitting means:
   - Each service owns its data; no cross-service joins or foreign keys.
   - You trade ACID transactions for eventual consistency and patterns like sagas/outbox.
   - Reporting/analytics that relied on joining everything gets much harder.
   
   If your pain is DB-bound, note you can often get a read replica, sharding, or a separate reporting DB *without* microservices.

**4. Operational maturity.** Microservices demand infrastructure a monolith doesn't: service discovery, centralized logging, distributed tracing, per-service CI/CD, container orchestration, monitoring/alerting, and a way to debug failures that span services. Do you have this, or the people to build and run it? This is where teams underestimate the cost most.

**5. Which problem are you actually solving?**
   - *Scaling one hot component* (e.g. a CPU-heavy endpoint) → you may only need to extract *that one piece*, not go full microservices.
   - *Deploy velocity / independent releases* → a valid microservices driver.
   - *Fault isolation* → valid, but adds network-failure modes you didn't have before.
   - *"It's slow"* → usually not a microservices problem (see above).

## The middle paths — usually the right move

You rarely have to choose between "keep the mess" and "full microservices":

- **Modular monolith first.** Enforce clean module boundaries *inside* the Django app (separate apps, explicit interfaces, no cross-module ORM reaching). This gives you most of the org/clarity benefits and makes any future extraction dramatically easier. Do this first regardless — it's the prerequisite for a clean split anyway.
- **Strangler fig.** If you do split, extract *one* well-bounded, painful service, learn the operational cost for real, then decide whether to continue. Never a big-bang rewrite.
- **Extract async workloads.** Move heavy/slow work to Celery workers — separate scaling and fault isolation without the distributed-systems tax on your request path.

## My recommendation

1. **Profile first.** Get real data on what's slow. Most "we need microservices" conversations end here.
2. **Fix the cheap wins** — queries, indexes, caching, async tasks.
3. **If you still need structural change**, do a modular monolith, then strangler-fig one service out and measure the true cost before committing the org to it.
