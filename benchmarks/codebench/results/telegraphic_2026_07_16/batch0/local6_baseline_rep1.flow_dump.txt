[assistant] Before I answer the microservices question directly — the framing worth challenging up front: **"the app is slow" is almost never a reason to go to microservices.** Microservices add network hops, serialization, and coordination overhead; they make most things *slower* per-request. What they buy you is independent scaling, deployment, and team autonomy — not speed. So the first question is whether you're solving the right problem.

## First: is "slow" actually a decomposition problem?

Profile before you re-architect. Common causes of a slow Django monolith that microservices won't fix (and may worsen):

- **N+1 queries / missing `select_related`/`prefetch_related`** — the single most common Django performance killer.
- **Missing DB indexes** or unbounded queries.
- **No caching layer** (per-view, template fragment, or `select`-level).
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — emails, report generation, third-party API calls. Moving these to Celery/task queue often reclaims most of your latency with a fraction of the effort of a rewrite.
- **N-workers/GIL saturation** — sometimes it's just under-provisioned WSGI/ASGI workers or a chatty ORM.

If you haven't exhausted these, do that first. You can get 5–10x on a monolith this way, cheaply and reversibly.

## If it's genuinely an architecture/scaling problem, the key factors:

**1. Team & organizational structure (Conway's Law)**
Microservices are primarily an *organizational* scaling tool. If you have 1–2 teams, service boundaries create coordination overhead without the payoff. The model works when independent teams own independent services. Are you actually team-bound, or code-bound?

**2. Can you find clean domain boundaries?**
Look for bounded contexts with low coupling and clear data ownership. If two candidate services would constantly need each other's data (chatty cross-service calls, distributed joins, shared tables), the boundary is wrong. A well-modularized monolith is a prerequisite — if you can't draw the lines cleanly *inside* the monolith, you can't draw them across the network.

**3. Data ownership and transactions**
This is the hardest part. Each service owning its own data means:
- You lose ACID transactions across boundaries → you need sagas / eventual consistency / compensating actions.
- Django's ORM, migrations, and admin assume one database. Splitting the DB is often more painful than splitting the code.
- Reporting/analytics that used to be a JOIN now needs aggregation across services.

**4. Operational maturity — do you have the plumbing?**
Microservices trade code complexity for operational complexity. Realistically you need: centralized logging, distributed tracing, per-service CI/CD, service discovery, containerization/orchestration, monitoring/alerting, and a way to run the whole system locally. If you don't have most of this, budget for building it *before* you see any benefit.

**5. Failure modes and latency**
In-process calls become network calls that can time out, fail partially, or arrive out of order. You now need retries, circuit breakers, idempotency, and graceful degradation. p99 latency usually goes *up*.

**6. Testing & local development**
End-to-end testing across services is dramatically harder than a monolith's test suite. Developer productivity often drops before it recovers.

## The pragmatic middle path

Most teams should reach for **modular monolith first**: enforce clear module boundaries, explicit interfaces between Django apps, no cross-module ORM reach-in, separate the read/write hot paths. This gives you 80% of the architectural discipline and makes a *later* extraction easy — you'll have already found the seams.

When you do extract, use the **strangler fig pattern**: peel off one well-bounded, genuinely-independent service (a good first candidate is something with different scaling needs or a clear async boundary — e.g. notifications, search, media processing), prove out your operational tooling on it, then decide whether to continue. Don't do a big-bang rewrite.
