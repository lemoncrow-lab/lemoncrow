[assistant] Good instinct to pause before splitting. Microservices solve *organizational* and *scaling* problems, but they trade code-level complexity for distributed-systems complexity — and a slow Django monolith is very often fixable without any of that pain. Here's how I'd think about it.

## First: is "microservices" even the right fix?

"The app is slow" is almost never a problem microservices solve well. Splitting a slow endpoint into a network call usually makes it *slower*. Before anything, figure out *why* it's slow:

- **Profile the actual bottlenecks.** Use `django-silk`, `django-debug-toolbar`, or APM (Datadog, Sentry Performance, New Relic). Nine times out of ten it's:
  - **N+1 queries** — fix with `select_related` / `prefetch_related`
  - **Missing DB indexes** — check slow query logs
  - **No caching** — Redis/Memcached for expensive reads
  - **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery (email, PDF gen, third-party calls)
  - **A single overloaded Postgres instance** — read replicas, connection pooling (PgBouncer)
- **Scale the monolith horizontally first.** Multiple gunicorn/uvicorn workers behind a load balancer gets you very far. A well-tuned Django monolith serves enormous traffic.

If you haven't done these, do them first. They're days of work, not quarters, and they don't introduce network partitions, distributed transactions, or a service mesh.

## If the drivers are real, evaluate these factors

**1. What's the actual motivation?**
- *Independent deployability* (teams blocked on each other's releases) → real microservices driver
- *Independent scaling* (one component needs 10x the resources) → could be a service, could be a queue
- *Team autonomy / Conway's Law* (you have multiple teams stepping on each other) → the strongest legitimate reason
- *Raw performance* → usually the wrong reason

**2. Do clean domain boundaries actually exist?**
Can you draw bounded contexts where data ownership is clean? If your models are a tangled web of foreign keys across every domain, you'll end up with a **distributed monolith** — all the network cost, none of the independence. This is the single most common failure mode. A good intermediate step: enforce boundaries *within* the monolith first (Django apps as modules with no cross-imports, or a "modular monolith") and see if the seams hold.

**3. Data and transactions.**
Monoliths give you ACID transactions for free. Split the DB and you lose that — cross-service consistency means sagas, eventual consistency, idempotency, and reconciliation logic. This is where most of the hidden cost lives.

**4. Operational maturity — be honest here.**
Microservices demand infrastructure you may not have:
- CI/CD per service, containerization/orchestration (K8s)
- Distributed tracing, centralized logging, per-service monitoring
- Service discovery, API gateway, inter-service auth
- On-call for N services instead of 1

If the team doesn't have this today, that's the project — not the split itself.

**5. Team size and topology.**
Microservices pay off when you have enough people that coordination *is* the bottleneck. A rough heuristic: if you have fewer than ~15–20 engineers, the monolith is probably still your friend. One service per team, not per developer.

## My recommendation

1. **Profile and fix the monolith first** — most slowness dies here.
2. **If org/scaling pressure is real, go modular monolith next** — clean module boundaries, enforced dependencies, single deploy. This gives you 80% of the architectural benefit and validates your domain boundaries at near-zero operational cost.
3. **Extract services surgically** — pull out the one component with a genuine independent-scaling or team-ownership need (often the async/worker layer, or a compute-heavy piece). Strangler-fig pattern, one seam at a time. Don't do a big-bang rewrite.

The question I'd put to the team: *"What can we do after splitting that we genuinely cannot do in the monolith?"* If the answer is "make it faster," the split is probably a detour. If the answer is "let five teams ship without coordinating," it may be time.
