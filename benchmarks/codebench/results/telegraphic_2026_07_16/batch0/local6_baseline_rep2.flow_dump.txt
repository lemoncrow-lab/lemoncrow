[assistant] This is a decision worth slowing down on, because "the monolith is slow" and "we should do microservices" are two separate claims — and the second rarely fixes the first. Here's how I'd frame it.

## First: is slowness actually an architecture problem?

Microservices don't make code faster — they add network hops, so raw latency usually gets *worse*. Before splitting, rule out the cheap wins:

- **Profile before you architect.** Is it N+1 queries, missing DB indexes, unbounded querysets, synchronous work that should be async (Celery), no caching layer? These are the usual culprits in a slow Django app and none require a service split.
- **Scaling axis.** If the app is CPU/throughput-bound, you often just need more Gunicorn workers / horizontal replicas behind a load balancer — the monolith scales horizontally fine if it's stateless.
- **Database is usually the real bottleneck.** And note: splitting services does *not* split the database for free. If everyone still hits one Postgres, you've added complexity without removing the constraint.

If profiling shows the problem is a hot path or a slow deploy/test cycle, fix that first. Microservices are an *organizational* solution more than a performance one.

## If it's genuinely a candidate for splitting, the key factors:

**1. Team & organizational structure (Conway's Law).**
Services map cleanly to teams. One team of 5? You'll feel the operational cost without the autonomy benefit. Multiple teams stepping on each other in the same deploy pipeline is the strongest *real* reason to split.

**2. Clear domain boundaries.**
Can you identify bounded contexts with low coupling between them (e.g. billing, notifications, search, auth)? If the domain model is a tangle of cross-cutting foreign keys, extraction will be painful and the seams will leak. Look for modules that already talk through narrow interfaces.

**3. Data ownership.**
Each service owning its own data is the whole point — and the hardest part. You lose cross-table JOINs and DB-level transactional integrity across boundaries; you inherit eventual consistency, distributed transactions / sagas, and data duplication. If two proposed services can't stop sharing tables, they're not two services.

**4. Operational maturity.**
Microservices are a tax you pay continuously: CI/CD per service, container orchestration, service discovery, centralized logging, distributed tracing, monitoring/alerting, secrets management. Do you have this, or the appetite to build it? Debugging a request across 5 services without tracing is miserable.

**5. Failure modes get harder.**
In-process calls become network calls that time out, retry, and cascade. You need circuit breakers, retries with backoff, idempotency, and graceful degradation. Overall availability can *drop* if services are chained synchronously.

**6. Team readiness & latency budget.**
Do engineers know async messaging, API versioning, contract testing? And can your product tolerate the added per-request latency of network hops?

## My recommended path

1. **Profile and fix the actual slowness first.** Often that ends the debate.
2. If boundaries and team scaling justify a split, go **modular monolith** first — enforce clean module boundaries *inside* the codebase (separate Django apps, no cross-module ORM reaching, explicit service interfaces). This gets you 80% of the decoupling with none of the ops tax.
3. Extract services **one at a time**, starting with the piece that has the clearest boundary and the strongest independent-scaling or independent-deploy need (a good first candidate is something like notifications, search, or a CPU-heavy worker).

The modular monolith is the underrated middle ground here — it validates your boundaries cheaply, and boundaries that survive as modules are the ones worth promoting to services later.
