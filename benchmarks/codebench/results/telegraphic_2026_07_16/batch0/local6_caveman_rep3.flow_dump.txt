[assistant] Good instinct to pause before splitting. Microservices trade code-level complexity for distributed-systems complexity — that trade only pays off under specific conditions. Here's what to weigh.

## First: is "microservices" even the right fix?

"Getting slow" is a performance problem. Microservices are an **organizational and scaling** solution, not a performance one — a network hop is slower than a function call. Splitting a slow monolith often gives you a slow *distributed* monolith that's harder to debug.

Before anything, profile and find the actual bottleneck:
- **N+1 queries** — the #1 Django killer. Check `django-debug-toolbar` / `nplusone`, add `select_related`/`prefetch_related`.
- **Missing DB indexes**, slow queries — `pg_stat_statements`.
- **No caching layer** — Redis/Memcached for hot reads.
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery.
- **N+1 on serialization**, oversized querysets, missing pagination.

Very often this reclaims the performance without touching architecture. Do this first regardless — you'll need to understand the bottlenecks anyway.

## If it's genuinely a scaling/org problem, the key factors

**1. Team & organizational readiness (Conway's Law)**
Services mirror team structure. A single team maintaining 10 services just gets a distributed monolith with more overhead. You want roughly one team owning each service, with clear ownership boundaries. How many teams do you actually have?

**2. Domain boundaries**
Can you cleanly separate domains (billing, auth, catalog, notifications)? If domains are deeply entangled — shared models, cross-cutting transactions everywhere — extraction is painful and premature. Look for natural seams with low coupling and high cohesion.

**3. Data ownership**
This is the hard part. Each service should own its data. That means:
- No shared database across services (or you've just moved the coupling).
- **Distributed transactions** — you lose ACID across service boundaries. Are you ready for eventual consistency, sagas, compensating transactions?
- Joins that were free SQL become network calls or data duplication.

**4. Operational maturity**
Microservices demand infra you may not have:
- Centralized logging + distributed tracing (you can't grep one log file anymore).
- Per-service CI/CD, containerization, orchestration (k8s or similar).
- Service discovery, API gateway, monitoring/alerting per service.
- On-call and incident response for a fleet, not one app.

If observability is weak today, distributed failures will be nearly impossible to debug.

**5. Independent scaling need**
The real win: if one component (e.g. image processing, search) needs very different scaling than the rest, isolating it lets you scale/deploy it independently. If everything scales together uniformly, that benefit doesn't apply.

**6. Deployment friction**
If a small change requires re-testing and redeploying the whole app, and that's blocking teams, independent deploys are a genuine payoff. Measure your current deploy pain honestly.

## Recommended path

1. **Fix the performance problems** in the monolith first (see above). Cheapest, highest ROI.
2. **Modularize inside the monolith** — enforce clean domain boundaries as Django apps with explicit interfaces, no cross-app model reach-through. This is the "modular monolith." You get most of the boundary benefits with none of the network cost.
3. **Extract only when a specific service has a concrete, measured reason** — different scaling profile, different team ownership, different tech need. Start with the most loosely-coupled, clearly-bounded piece (e.g. notifications, image processing) as a trial.

Don't do a big-bang rewrite. If you extract, use the **strangler fig** pattern — peel off one service at a time while the monolith keeps running.

## The honest summary

Most "we need microservices" conversations are actually "our monolith is poorly structured and under-optimized." Microservices don't fix a messy codebase — they distribute the mess across a network. Earn the split with a modular monolith and a concrete scaling/ownership reason, not a general feeling of slowness.
