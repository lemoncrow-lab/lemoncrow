[assistant] Before splitting a monolith, the most important thing to establish is whether microservices actually address your problem. "Slow" and "monolith" are often unrelated — so I'd frame the decision around these factors:

## 1. Diagnose *why* it's slow first
This is the big one. Microservices rarely fix performance; they usually add latency (network hops, serialization). Before any split, profile:
- **N+1 queries / missing indexes** — the usual culprit in Django. Check `django-debug-toolbar` / `django-silk`.
- **Missing caching** — Redis/memcached for expensive queries, template fragments, `select_related`/`prefetch_related`.
- **Synchronous work that should be async** — offload to Celery (emails, reports, third-party calls).
- **N+1 at the ORM level, unbounded querysets, serialization overhead in DRF.**

If the slowness is a handful of hot endpoints or bad queries, you can often get 10x from a week of profiling — vastly cheaper than a rearchitecture.

## 2. Is it a *scaling* problem or an *architecture* problem?
- If the app is slow because one component (e.g. video processing) starves everything else → you might extract *just that* into a service or worker, not shatter the whole thing.
- If it's slow because you can't scale horizontally → often solvable by running more Django processes/pods behind a load balancer, plus a read replica. Django scales horizontally fine if it's stateless.

## 3. Organizational readiness (Conway's Law)
Microservices are primarily an **organizational** solution, not a performance one. They pay off when multiple teams need to deploy independently. Ask:
- How many teams/engineers? Below ~15-20 engineers, microservices usually cost more than they return.
- Do you have the ops maturity? Distributed systems need: centralized logging, distributed tracing, CI/CD per service, service discovery, on-call for many deployables.

## 4. Domain boundaries
If you do split, the boundaries must follow **business domains** (bounded contexts), not technical layers. The hardest part is data:
- Which tables are shared? Cross-service joins become API calls or data duplication.
- Are your models a tangled web of foreign keys? If you can't cleanly separate the data, you'll build a **distributed monolith** — all the pain, none of the benefits.

## 5. Data consistency & transactions
The monolith gives you free ACID transactions. Across services you lose that and inherit eventual consistency, sagas, idempotency, and distributed failure handling. This is a major complexity jump.

## 6. The cost side
- More infra, more network latency, harder local dev, harder debugging across boundaries, versioning of APIs, data migrations across services.

---

## My recommendation
**Don't jump to microservices to fix speed.** A pragmatic sequence:

1. **Profile and fix the hot paths** (queries, caching, Celery). Usually solves "slow."
2. **Scale horizontally** + add a DB read replica if it's load-related.
3. **If** the codebase/team is genuinely too big to coordinate, move to a **modular monolith** first — enforce clean domain boundaries *inside* the same codebase (Django apps with strict interfaces, no cross-app model imports). This gives you most of the boundary discipline with none of the distributed-systems tax.
4. **Extract a service only when a specific module has a proven, independent reason** — different scaling profile, different language need, or a team that must deploy on its own cadence. Strangler-fig pattern, one seam at a time.

If you can share what your profiling shows (slow endpoints, DB time vs. Python time, request volume, team size), I can get much more specific about whether a split is even the right lever.
