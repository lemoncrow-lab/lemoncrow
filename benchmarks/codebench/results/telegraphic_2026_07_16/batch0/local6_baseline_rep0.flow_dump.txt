[assistant] Good instinct to pause before splitting — microservices are usually the wrong first response to "the monolith is slow," and they trade a performance problem you can profile for a distributed-systems problem you can't. Here's how I'd think about it.

## First: is "slow" even a decomposition problem?

Before anything architectural, find out *why* it's slow. Nearly always the culprit is one of these, none of which microservices fix:

- **N+1 queries / missing `select_related`/`prefetch_related`** — the classic Django killer. Turn on `django-debug-toolbar` or log queries per request.
- **Missing DB indexes** or slow queries — check `pg_stat_statements`.
- **No caching layer** — Redis/Memcached for hot reads, template fragment caching.
- **Synchronous work in the request path** that belongs in Celery (emails, PDF generation, third-party API calls).
- **Undersized DB or a single web worker** — sometimes it's just capacity.

If you split into services *without* fixing these, you get the same slow queries plus network latency between services. **You must be able to point at a profiled bottleneck that decomposition actually addresses** (e.g., one CPU-bound subsystem starving everything else) — otherwise this is a performance-tuning task, not an architecture one.

## If it's genuinely an architecture/scaling problem, the key factors

**1. Team & organizational readiness (Conway's Law)**
Microservices are an org pattern first, a tech pattern second. Do you have multiple teams that can own services independently? One team maintaining five services just has a distributed monolith with more pager duty. Who owns deploys, on-call, and cross-service incidents?

**2. Service boundaries / domain seams**
Can you actually draw clean bounded contexts? If modules are tangled (shared models, circular imports, everything touching `User`), you can't cut cleanly. The honest test: can you first split it into well-isolated Django apps *inside* the monolith with no cross-imports? If you can't do it in-process, you definitely can't do it over a network.

**3. Data ownership and the shared database**
This is where most Django splits die. Each service owning its own data means:
- No more cross-table `JOIN`s — they become API calls or data duplication.
- No more ACID transactions across boundaries — you need sagas/eventual consistency.
- The Django ORM's biggest ergonomic wins evaporate at service edges.
If two prospective services can't stop sharing tables, they're one service.

**4. Operational maturity**
Do you have: CI/CD per service, centralized logging, distributed tracing (OpenTelemetry), metrics/alerting, service discovery, container orchestration? Debugging a request across services without tracing is brutal. If you don't have this today, budget for building it *before* you get value.

**5. Latency & consistency budget**
In-process calls are nanoseconds; network calls are milliseconds and *can fail*. Every split adds latency, retries, timeouts, and partial-failure states you must now handle explicitly. Some flows tolerate eventual consistency; some (payments, inventory) don't.

**6. Cost — real and honest**
More infra, more monitoring, more deploy pipelines, and a real productivity dip while the team learns distributed debugging. This is a multi-quarter investment, not a sprint.

## The path I'd actually recommend

1. **Profile and fix the real bottleneck** — you may buy a year of runway in a week.
2. **Modularize in place** — enforce clean app boundaries, kill cross-app model imports, introduce internal service interfaces. This gets you 80% of the maintainability benefit with none of the network cost, and it's the prerequisite for any future split.
3. **Scale the monolith** — horizontal web workers behind a load balancer, read replicas, Celery for async, caching. Django scales further than people expect (Instagram is a Django monolith).
4. **If one subsystem has genuinely different scaling or team-ownership needs, extract *that one service*** — a strangler-fig extraction of the worst-fit module. Don't Big Bang the whole thing.

**Rule of thumb:** extract a service when the pain of it being *inside* the monolith (independent scaling, independent deploys, separate team ownership, different tech needs) clearly exceeds the distributed-systems tax. "It's slow" almost never meets that bar on its own; "this one module needs to scale 10x independently and a different team owns it" does.

Want me to dig into your codebase — profile the slow endpoints, check for N+1s and indexing, or map out where the natural module seams are? That's usually the highest-leverage next step.
